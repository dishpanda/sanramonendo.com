Root Canal Treatment

What is a Root Canal? The technical terminology for Root Canal is Endodontic Treatment. “Endo” is the Greek word for “inside” and “odont” is Greek for “tooth.” Endodontic treatment treats the inside of the tooth. Root canal treatment is just one type of endodontic treatment.

To understand Root Canal treatment, it helps to know something about the anatomy of the tooth. Inside the tooth, under the white enamel there is a hard layer called the dentin. Inside the dentin is a soft tissue called the pulp. The pulp contains blood vessels, nerves, and connective tissue. The pulp extends from the crown of the tooth to the tip of the root.

When the pulp becomes irreversibly inflamed, or necrotic (dies), root canal treatment may save the tooth otherwise destined for extraction. Endodontic treatment (a Root Canal) can often be performed in one visit and involves the following steps:

After the endodontist makes a diagnosis indicating treatment is necessary, local anesthetic is administered. Only after being absolutely certain the tooth is numb, a protective sheet called a “dental dam” is placed to isolate the tooth and shield the oral cavity from debris and water spray from the operating hand-piece (dental drill).

An access hole is then made in the biting surface of the tooth. Next, very small instruments are used to remove the diseased pulp tissue from the pulp space (root canal space), and shape the canal in preparation for canal filling. The prepared root canal is filled with a bio-compatible material called gutta-percha. Then, together with a special cement, the canal is sealed using a warm instrument.

Typically, a temporary filling is placed in the access opening to protect the root canal treatment from contamination by oral bacteria until the general dentist places the final restoration. After the final visit with your endodontist, you must return to your general dentist to have a crown or other restoration placed on the tooth to protect and restore it to full function.

Adult Sedation Endodontics

Although 95% of our patients undergo endodontic procedures with the use of local anesthetic injection to provided complete anesthesia and a comfortable endodontic experience, some individuals may require specialized procedures.

Dr. Saoji has been trained, and is licensed to provide oral sedation for adults. Using safe and effective well-established protocols, the desired relaxed and comfortable state can be achieved in most cases.

For adult patients with certain specific conditions, in addition to local anesthetic injection, we offer three different levels of anxiety management. These include:

  1. Nitrous Oxide:Oxygen administration:

    2. A mask covers the nose and mixture of nitrous oxide and oxygen enter the body via the nasal passage, and is absorbed by the lungs. The mixture of gas is carefully titrated to provide a relaxed state in preparation for anesthetic injection. There are certain limitations, but the technique, though adding time to the procedure, does not require a driver to take the patient home.

  2. Nitrous Oxide: Oxygen in combination with Anti-Anxiety Medication:

    3. In addition to the above, anti-anxiety medication is taken at bedtime the night before the endodontic treatment appointment, and then again one hour before the scheduled treatment visit. Equipment to monitor vital signs is attached to the arm and finger, a driver must accompany you to and from the visit, and a sedation consultation appointment is required.

Endodontic Microsurgery

It is possible that a nonsurgical root canal procedure will not be enough to save your tooth that is otherwise destined for extraction. If so, there is one last procedure that may accomplish the goal of saving the tooth. That is Endodontic Surgery.

This minor surgical procedure can be used to locate small fractures or hidden canals at the root apex (the tip of the tooth root) previously undetected during the initial root canal treatment. Surgery may also be needed to remove calcium deposits in root canals, or to treat damaged root surfaces or the surrounding bone of the tooth. Unusual root end anatomy, or bacterial contamination, are possible reasons the initial treatment did not work as expected. These conditions can be addressed with endodontic surgery.

The procedure is much less traumatic then having a tooth extracted. And, if performed on a Friday morning, the patient is usually ready for work by the following Monday. There are restrictions during first 24 hours after surgery, and your endodontist may prescribe medication, but by the second day after treatment patients are usually up and about performing their usual activities.

Endodontic surgery is performed using advanced technologies including, digital imaging, operating microscopes, and ultrasonic micro-instrumentation.

An apicoectomy, or root-end resection, is the most common endodontic surgery undergone. Your endodontist performs this micro-surgical procedure by opening the gum tissue near the tooth to see the underlying bone and to remove any inflamed or infected tissue. The very end of the root is removed, and a small filling may be placed to seal the end of the root canal. Finally, the gum tissue is closed using a few sutures.

Post-surgical discomfort is generally mild,and is easily managed with medication the endodontist will prescribe.
